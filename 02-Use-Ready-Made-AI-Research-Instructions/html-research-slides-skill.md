# Skill: Interactive HTML Research Slides

## Purpose

Create a shareable, interactive HTML slide deck for an economics or finance research talk, teaching session, or public-facing research explanation.

This skill is designed for talks where interactivity, animation, web sharing, or visual explanation helps the audience understand the research.

## Best For

- seminar explainers
- online workshop material
- interactive mechanism diagrams
- public research summaries
- teaching modules
- project websites
- conference talks that can use browser-based slides

## Not Suitable For

- strict conference PowerPoint templates
- confidential talks that cannot be shared online
- rooms where browser support is uncertain
- coauthors who need to edit slides only in PowerPoint
- talks where animation distracts from evidence

## Required Inputs

Provide:

- paper title
- research question
- audience and talk length
- main contribution
- mechanism or conceptual diagram
- identification strategy or model structure
- 3 to 5 main results
- figures/tables to include
- required disclaimers or confidentiality constraints
- preferred style: playful-interactive, clean-academic, data-dashboard, or public-explainer
- output target: single HTML file, folder with assets, or HTML plus PDF export

## Optional Inputs

- existing PPT/PPTX
- paper draft
- abstract
- speaker notes
- preferred color palette
- institutional branding constraints
- deployment target

## Workflow

1. Confirm privacy constraints before using any unpublished or restricted content.
2. Extract the research story: question, why it matters, method, evidence, contribution.
3. Build a slide outline before generating HTML.
4. Choose one visual style based on audience and research content.
5. Generate slides with stable layout, readable typography, and restrained animation.
6. Use interactivity only to clarify mechanism, data, identification, or results.
7. Check every number, label, figure, and causal claim.
8. Export or deploy only after coauthor and confidentiality review.

## Output Requirements

- no generic AI-gradient aesthetic
- no decorative animation without research purpose
- readable on laptop and projector
- figures keep labels, units, legends, and sources
- speaker notes clearly separated from slide text
- all illustrative graphics labeled as illustrative
- HTML source should be understandable enough to edit

## Verification Checklist

- Are all results copied correctly?
- Are all figure axes and legends correct?
- Does any animation distract from evidence?
- Does the deck overstate identification or policy relevance?
- Does the deck expose unpublished or restricted material?
- Is there a static PDF fallback?
